Jean-Jacques Rousseau (1712–1778) was a Swiss-born philosopher, writer, and composer whose ideas profoundly influenced the Enlightenment, the French Revolution, and modern political thought.

Early Life

Born in Geneva, Rousseau had a turbulent upbringing. After leaving home, he traveled across Europe before settling in Paris, where he became a significant figure in intellectual circles.

Philosophical Contributions

Rousseau’s most famous works include "The Social Contract" and "Emile, or On Education". He believed that humans were inherently good but became corrupted by society. His ideas on freedom, equality, and governance laid the groundwork for many modern democratic principles.

Influence and Legacy

Rousseau’s philosophy shaped the intellectual landscape of the 18th century, influencing revolutionary movements and continuing to inspire discussions in education, politics, and human rights.
